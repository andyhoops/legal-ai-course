# Facilitator Session Guide

This guide is for the coach running sessions with the student. It covers the rhythm of each session, how to get the most out of the AI-facilitated format, and what to watch for as the course progresses.

---

## Your role

You are not the teacher. Claude is not the teacher either. The student is the learner, and the learning happens in the interaction between them.

Your job is to:
- **Hold the space** — show up, be present, care about what they discover
- **Keep the structure** — make sure sessions happen, journal entries get written, portfolio gets built
- **Ask the interesting question** — the opening question each week is yours to deliver; make it feel genuine, not scripted
- **Celebrate wins** — not just "well done" but "that was a really sharp question" or "I hadn't thought of it that way"
- **Notice the stuck moments** — if the student looks frustrated or disengaged, name it and explore it together

You don't need to know contract law. You don't need to know how Claude works. You need to be a thoughtful adult who takes their intellectual development seriously.

---

## Session structure (60–90 minutes)

### Opening (5–10 min)
- Check in briefly: how are they? what's on their mind?
- Deliver the week's opening question — as a genuine question, not a quiz
- Give them 2–3 minutes to think before Claude enters the picture

### Main session (40–60 min)
- The student drives — they open Claude, they write the prompts
- Your role: observe, and occasionally prompt with "what would happen if you asked it that?" or "what don't you understand yet about what Claude just said?"
- Resist the urge to take over

### Tool-building or output time (10–15 min)
- Weeks with a specific artefact to produce (a prompt, a case analysis, a CLAUDE.md)
- Help the student think about what they want to save and why

### Journal entry (10 min)
- Non-negotiable: every session ends with a journal entry, however short
- Give them space to write — don't hover
- When they're done, ask: "what's the one thing you want to remember from today?"

### Close (5 min)
- Preview next week briefly
- Confirm the journal entry is saved to GitHub
- Note anything you want to carry forward

## Month-by-month priorities

### Month 1: Curiosity
- Don't correct. Don't over-explain. Let Claude do the heavy lifting.
- Your job: make sure the student is genuinely curious, not just going through the motions
- GitHub account set up by end of Week 1. First commit by end of Week 4.

### Month 2: Rigour
- This is where you introduce the "what if Claude is wrong?" question
- Encourage the student to push back — model it yourself if needed: "I'd want to double-check that. How would we verify it?"
- GitHub repo live with Month 1 journal entries by end of Week 5

### Month 3: Ownership
- Step back more. Let the student direct the session.
- By Week 12 the student should be running the research workflow with minimal prompting from you
- Check: does their CLAUDE.md reflect *their* thinking about their project, or is it just what Claude suggested?

### Month 4: Presentation
- Read their writing. Give honest feedback. "This is good" is less useful than "this paragraph made me want to read the next one."
- Week 16 sign-off: consider doing a mini-presentation — the student presents their portfolio to you (or another adult). 20 minutes, then questions. This builds real confidence.

---

## Signs the course is working

- The student corrects Claude before you do
- Journal entries get more specific over time (less "I learned about consideration" and more "I still don't understand why past consideration is different from executed consideration — Claude's explanation didn't fully convince me")
- They bring their own questions to sessions, not just follow the structure
- They talk about legal concepts in conversation, unprompted

## Signs to watch for

- Sessions becoming passive — student watching Claude rather than driving it
- Journal entries becoming summaries rather than reflections (they've described *what* but not *what it meant*)
- Loss of momentum — the course is long; if energy dips in Month 2 or 3, a shorter, more playful session can reset things

## Practical notes

- **Platform:** Claude Code (claude.ai or CLI). No coding required — the student can use the web interface throughout.
- **The student's repo:** Help set it up in Week 1, but after that they own it. They should be the ones pushing commits.
- **This repo (student-legal-project):** This is *your* facilitator planning repo. The student doesn't need to see it.
- **Time:** 60 minutes is the minimum viable session. 90 minutes is ideal. Under 45 minutes and there's not enough space for real depth.
- **Flexibility:** The week-by-week structure is a guide, not a contract. If a case or topic catches fire and the student wants to go deeper, let them. The structure will wait.
