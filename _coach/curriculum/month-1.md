# Month 1 — Foundations
*"Meeting your AI partner and your legal toolkit"*

## What this month is about

Month 1 is about getting comfortable — with Claude, with contract law, and with the idea that asking good questions is a skill worth developing. There's no pressure to produce polished work yet. The goal is curiosity.

By the end of Month 1 the student will:
- Have had four sessions with Claude Code
- Know the basic building blocks of a contract under English law
- Have written their first structured legal research prompts
- Have made their first four journal entries
- Have a GitHub account set up

## Week 1 — What Is This? What Is AI?

**Opening question for the student:** *If you had to explain to a friend what a contract is, what would you say?*

### Legal content
- What makes something a legally binding contract?
- The three core elements: offer, acceptance, consideration (introduce gently — details come in later weeks)
- Why contract law matters: it's the foundation of almost every transaction

### AI skill: Your first conversation
- Open Claude Code and say hello
- Ask Claude to explain what a contract is "as if I'm 16 and haven't studied law"
- Notice: how does Claude's answer compare to your instinct? What did it add? What felt off?
- Try changing the prompt slightly — "now explain it to a first-year law student" — and compare

### Session activities
1. Have a free-flowing conversation with Claude about what a contract is
2. Ask Claude: "What are the most important contract law cases a student should know?"
3. Write your first journal entry (use the template in `student/journal-template.md`)

### Journal prompt
> "What surprised you most about your first conversation with Claude? Did it feel like talking to a person? A search engine? Something else?"

### Facilitator notes
- Keep this session loose and exploratory — resist the urge to correct or direct too much
- The point is to get the student noticing and wondering, not to deliver a lecture
- GitHub account creation: help them choose a professional username (FirstnameSurname or FirstnameInitial are good)

## Week 2 — Offer and Acceptance

**Opening question:** *If I shout "£5 to whoever brings me a coffee!" and someone brings one, do I have to pay?*

### Legal content
- Offer vs invitation to treat (*Fisher v Bell* [1961], *Pharmaceutical Society v Boots* [1953])
- Acceptance: must be communicated, mirror image rule
- The postal rule (an oddity worth knowing)
- Case anchor: ***Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co* [1893]**
  - The advertisement, the promise, the flu, the refusal to pay
  - Why this case is still studied 130 years later

### AI skill: Writing a clear, specific prompt
- Compare a vague prompt ("tell me about offer and acceptance") with a specific one
- Introduce the idea of **role, task, context, format** as a simple prompt framework
- Try: "You are a law tutor. Explain the rule from *Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co* and then give me three modern examples of where the same principle would apply."

### Session activities
1. Ask Claude to explain *Carlill* — first vaguely, then with the RTCF framework
2. Ask Claude: "What are the three strongest arguments the company tried to use in *Carlill* and why did they fail?"
3. Try to write a prompt that gets Claude to produce a case summary you'd actually want to keep

### Journal prompt
> "The company in *Carlill* argued the advertisement was 'mere puff' — not a real legal offer. Do you find that argument convincing? Why or why not? What does Claude think, and do you agree?"

## Week 3 — Consideration

**Opening question:** *If I promise to give you my old laptop for free, can you sue me if I change my mind?*

### Legal content
- Consideration: the "price" of a promise
- Must be sufficient but need not be adequate (*Chappell & Co v Nestlé* [1960] — three wrappers)
- Past consideration is not good consideration
- Promissory estoppel (brief introduction — the exception to the rule)
- *Williams v Roffey Bros* [1991] — can a promise to pay more be enforceable?

### AI skill: Adding context to prompts
- Experiment with giving Claude background before asking the main question
- Try: start the conversation by explaining who you are and what you're trying to learn, then ask your question
- Notice whether Claude's answer changes when it has more context

### Session activities
1. Ask Claude to explain consideration using only examples from everyday life (no legal jargon)
2. Explore the *Chappell v Nestlé* case: "Why did the House of Lords decide that chocolate wrappers counted as consideration? What are the limits of that reasoning?"
3. Ask Claude to compare *Chappell v Nestlé* with *Williams v Roffey* — what do the two cases have in common?

### Journal prompt
> "The consideration doctrine says a promise only becomes enforceable once there's a 'price' for it. Does that seem fair to you? Can you think of a situation where it would produce an unfair result?"

## Week 4 — Intention and the First Analysis

**Opening question:** *If your mum promises to give you £10 for cleaning your room and then doesn't, can you take her to court?*

### Legal content
- Intention to create legal relations — the missing element that students often forget
- Domestic agreements: *Balfour v Balfour* [1919] — the husband's allowance, the presumption against enforcement
- The presumption rebutted: *Merritt v Merritt* [1970]
- Commercial agreements: the opposite presumption applies

### AI skill: Asking Claude to structure an answer
- Introduce the IRAC framework (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) — the backbone of legal analysis
- Ask Claude to analyse a scenario using IRAC
- Try to get Claude to produce something structured enough that you could submit it as coursework

### Session activities
1. Ask Claude to explain *Balfour v Balfour* — then ask "What would need to be different for Mrs Balfour to have won?"
2. Give Claude a hypothetical and ask for an IRAC analysis: "My friend and I agreed to split the cost of a concert ticket. She hasn't paid me back. Do I have a contract claim?"
3. Attempt your first structured case analysis on *Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co* using IRAC, with Claude as your thinking partner

### Journal prompt
> "Looking back at the four weeks: what is one thing you understand about contract law now that you didn't before? And what is one thing that still confuses you?"

### Facilitator notes
- This week introduces the first real analytical task — the IRAC analysis
- Don't expect perfection: the goal is for the student to experience the structure, not master it
- The journal entry this week should be a genuine reflection, not a summary of the law
- **End-of-month check:** The student should now have: GitHub account, 4 journal entries, at least one saved prompt they're proud of
