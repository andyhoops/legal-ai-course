# Month 4 — Portfolio
*"Showing the world what you've learned"*

## What this month is about

Month 4 is about going public. Everything built over three months — the journal entries, the prompt library, the case analyses, the tools — gets polished and presented. The student writes for an audience beyond themselves and emerges with something they're genuinely proud to share.

By the end of Month 4 the student will:
- Have a live GitHub Pages site documenting their learning journey
- Have completed a capstone legal analysis project
- Have a polished GitHub repo that a law school admissions tutor could read
- Have a reflective piece about their experience with AI as a learning partner
- Be able to describe — confidently and honestly — what they can do with AI

---

## Week 13 — GitHub and Writing for an Audience

**Opening question:** *Who might read your GitHub page? What do you want them to think?*

### Legal content
- None new — this week is about synthesis and communication
- Review which legal topics the student feels most confident explaining to others

### AI skill: Setting up GitHub Pages
- Configure GitHub Pages from the student's repo (simple — just enabling it and choosing a theme)
- Write the public README: who you are, what this project is, what you learned
- Ask Claude to help draft and then critique the README: "Does this make a stranger want to keep reading?"

### Session activities
1. Enable GitHub Pages on the student's repo
2. Draft the public README with Claude's help — aim for something honest, specific, and interesting (not a list of topics covered)
3. Write a short "about this project" page that explains the learning journey to a complete stranger

### Journal prompt
> "What's the hardest part about explaining what you've learned to someone who wasn't there? What do you most want a reader to take away from your GitHub page?"

## Week 14 — Documenting Your Journey

**Opening question:** *What does intellectual growth actually look like? How do you show it?*

### Legal content
- None new — reflection week

### AI skill: Writing reflections in public
- How to write a learning journal entry that's honest, specific, and interesting to read
- The difference between summarising what happened and reflecting on what it meant
- Ask Claude to review a journal entry and suggest how to make it more compelling

### Session activities
1. Review all 13 previous journal entries — identify the three moments of biggest growth
2. Write an expanded reflection on one of those moments (500–700 words) — this becomes a featured post on the GitHub Pages site
3. Ask Claude to play the role of a law school admissions tutor and give feedback on the reflection

### Journal prompt
> "Looking back at your first journal entry: what did you not know then that you know now? What are you still uncertain about — and why is that uncertainty okay?"

## Week 15 — Capstone Project

**Opening question:** *What legal question genuinely interests you? What would you most like to understand better?*

### Legal content
- Student-chosen topic within UK contract law
- Suggestions if they want one:
  - "Was the decision in *Williams v Roffey* right?" (consideration reform)
  - "Does the Consumer Rights Act 2015 go far enough?" (consumer protection)
  - "Should remoteness of damage be abolished?" (remedies reform)
  - "Analyse the contract in [a real-world event they care about]" (practical application)

### AI skill: Full research workflow — solo run
- The student runs the entire research workflow themselves, with Claude as partner but without facilitator direction
- They choose the question, plan the approach, use their tools, and produce the output
- The facilitator's role is to observe and celebrate

### Session activities
1. Define the capstone question (student's choice, agreed with facilitator)
2. Run the full research workflow — using the case analysis template, the prompt library, the CLAUDE.md
3. Produce the final output: a structured legal essay or analysis (600–900 words) suitable for the portfolio

### Journal prompt
> "You just completed a piece of legal research almost entirely under your own direction, using AI as your partner. Describe how that felt compared to Week 1. What has changed?"

## Week 16 — Final Polish and Sign-Off

**Opening question:** *If you were recommending this learning journey to a friend, what would you tell them?*

### Legal content
- None new — celebration and consolidation

### AI skill: Portfolio review
- Ask Claude to audit the GitHub repo: "What's missing? What could be clearer? What would make this more impressive?"
- Make final refinements to the GitHub Pages site
- Write the final reflective piece: "What I learned about AI, law, and learning itself"

### Session activities
1. Final repo audit with Claude — fix any gaps or rough edges
2. Write the closing reflective piece — honest, specific, personal
3. **Sign-off:** facilitator reviews the full portfolio with the student; celebrate what was built

### Final portfolio checklist

- [ ] Live GitHub Pages site with clear navigation
- [ ] A compelling public README
- [ ] 16 weekly journal entries (at minimum, 14+)
- [ ] Prompt library with 10+ annotated prompts
- [ ] 3–5 case study analyses
- [ ] 1 extended reflection piece
- [ ] Capstone legal analysis (600–900 words)
- [ ] CLAUDE.md in the repo
- [ ] Closing reflection: "What I learned"

### Journal prompt (final)
> "This is the last journal entry of the course. Write it to your future self: what do you know now about AI, about law, and about how you learn — that you want to remember in five years' time?"

### Facilitator notes
- Week 16 is a milestone — mark it appropriately
- The student should leave this session with a link they can put on a CV or application form *today*
- Consider: a short "viva" where the student presents their portfolio to you (or another adult) — this builds confidence and simulates a real presentation context
- If the student wants to continue: suggest a Month 5 stretch project — reading a real contract (tenancy agreement, employment contract) and producing a professional-grade analysis
